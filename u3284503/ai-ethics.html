Ethics

Correct AI governance is a major concern of our time, and the 2nd Global Forum on the Ethics of AI: Changing the Landscape of AI Governance was discussed in February 2024 at the Brdo Congress Centre of Kranj. In order to foster a targeted exchange for mutual learning, the Forum brought together experiences and skills from countries with differing degrees of technology and policy development. It provided a forum for discussion between the business community, academic institutions, and larger civil society groups, showcasing the global lessons and best practices in AI governance.

These quick developments do, however, also have important ethical ramifications. Among other things, AI systems may inadvertently introduce prejudices, exacerbate environmental damage, and infringe human rights. These dangers connected to AI have the potential to worsen already-existing disparities, hurting previously marginalized populations even more.

A Human Rights Approach to AI

In November 2021, UNESCO issued a ground-breaking AI ethics standard known as the 'Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence'. The primary foundation of this framework is the protection of human rights and dignity, which has been adopted by all 193 UNESCO Member States. It highlights ten core principles:

Principles Explanation
1. Proportionality and Do No Harm Artificial intelligence (AI) systems should only be used as much as is required to accomplish a valid goal. Risk assessment should to be utilized to stop any potential harm from these kinds of applications.

2. Safety and Security AI agents should take precautions against unwanted consequences (safety risks) and attack weaknesses (security risks).

3. Right to Privacy and Data Protection Protection and promotion of privacy are crucial throughout the AI lifecycle. It is also necessary to set up adequate structures for data protection.

4. Multi-stakeholder and Adaptive Governance and Collaboration When using data, one must comply with both national and international law. Additionally, inclusive methods to AI governance require the involvement of a variety of stakeholders.

5. Responsibility and Accountability AI programs ought to be traceable and auditable. Mechanisms for oversight, impact assessments, audits, and due diligence should be in place to prevent violations of human rights laws and dangers to the health of the environment.

6. Transparency and Explainability AI systems' ethical use is contingent upon their transparency and explainability (TandE). There may be conflicts between TandE and other values like privacy, safety, and security, thus the level of TandE should be suitable for the situation.

7. Human Oversight and Determination Member states needs to make sure that human accountability and ultimate responsibility are not replaced by AI systems.

8. Sustainability AI innovations should be evaluated in light of their effects on "sustainability," which is defined as a collection of dynamic objectives that includes the Sustainable Development Goals of the United Nations.

9. Awareness and Literacy Promoting civic engagement, digital skills and AI ethics training, media and information literacy, open and accessible education, and public knowledge of AI and data are all important.
